How Our Cron Expression Evaluator Works

This tool takes a cron expression (either the classic 5‑field Linux format or the 6‑field version used by AWS, Quartz, etc.) and computes the next several execution times based on the current time. It also validates the syntax, translates the expression into plain English, and estimates how often the job would run over a year.

Everything runs locally in your browser – the expression is never sent to a server, so you can safely test crontab fragments from sensitive environments.

Cron Expression Example

Take a practical example: */15 8-17 * * 1-5. This expression means “every 15 minutes, between 8 AM and 5 PM, Monday through Friday”. Paste it into the evaluator and you'll see the next runs — e.g., at 08:00, 08:15, 08:30 … 17:45 — and the tool estimates it fires about 2,080 times per year.

You can try common patterns such as 0 0 * * * (daily at midnight), 0 9 * * 1 (every Monday at 9 AM), or */5 * * * * (every 5 minutes).

Cron Expression Meaning

A cron expression is a string of five or six fields that define when a job runs. Here's how they break down in the standard format:

  • Minute (0–59) — when within the hour
  • Hour (0–23) — the hour of the day
  • Day of month (1–31) — which day
  • Month (1–12) — which month
  • Day of week (0–7, where both 0 and 7 mean Sunday)

Each field can contain an asterisk (* meaning “every”), a specific number, a range (1-5), a step (*/5), or a comma‑separated list (1,15,30). The extended format adds a leading Second field (0‑59).

Our evaluator decodes these fields and shows you exactly when the job will fire, removing the guesswork.

Common Cron Expression Examples

Here are some frequently used expressions and what they do:

Expression Meaning

0 0 1 * * First day of every month at midnight
0 0 * * 0 Every Sunday at midnight
@daily Same as 0 0 * * *

Usage Tips

  • The Standard format uses five fields: minute, hour, day of month, month, day of week.
  • The Extended format adds an initial seconds field (0‑59).
  • You can use macros such as @hourly, @daily, @weekly, @monthly, @yearly.
  • Supported patterns: *, */n, n-m, n,m, and mixed ranges.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does a cron expression evaluator do?

It parses a cron expression, checks if it's valid, and then calculates the exact dates and times when the job will fire. This is useful for debugging, validating, or simply understanding a schedule before you deploy it.

How many future runs does this evaluator show?

By default it displays up to 20 upcoming executions. You can adjust the number if you need more or fewer samples.

Does it handle both seconds and milliseconds?

The evaluator works with minute‑ or second‑based cron expressions. If you select the extended format, the first field is interpreted as seconds.
