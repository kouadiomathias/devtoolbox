Understanding UUID collision probability

UUIDs (Universally Unique Identifiers) are designed to be unique, but they are not infinite. The chance of two randomly generated UUIDs colliding is extremely low, yet not zero. The probability follows the birthday problem: with n identifiers and d possible values, the approximate probability of at least one collision is:

p(n) ≈ 1 − exp(−n² / (2d))

For UUID v4, d = 2122 (approximately 5.3 × 1036) because 6 bits are reserved for version and variant. This means you would need to generate about 2.7 × 1018 UUIDs to have a 50% chance of a single collision. That's 2.7 billion billion.

Collision probability for common identifiers

Use the calculator to see how different identifier types compare:

  • UUID v7 – same 122 bits, but time‑ordered; collision probability identical to v4 for the random part.
  • NanoID (21 chars) – 126 bits of entropy with default alphabet (64 symbols).
  • ULID – 80 bits of randomness, 48‑bit timestamp; total 128 bits, but the randomness is 80 bits for uniqueness.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can two UUIDs ever be the same?

Yes, theoretically it's possible, but the probability is astronomically small. With 122 random bits, you need to generate about 2.7 × 1018 UUIDs to have a 50% chance of collision. For comparison, generating 1 billion UUIDs per second for 100 years would produce only 3.2 × 1018 UUIDs.

Are UUID v4 collisions a real concern in practice?

No. The number of UUIDs needed for a meaningful collision probability is so large that it's not a practical concern for any real‑world system. Other failure modes (e.g., database corruption, bugs) are far more likely.

How does UUID v7 compare to v4 in terms of collisions?

UUID v7 has the same random part size (74 bits after the timestamp), so the collision probability is identical to v4 for the random portion. The timestamp ensures ordering but does not increase the random entropy.
